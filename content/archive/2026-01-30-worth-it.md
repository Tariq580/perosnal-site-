Everyone says I wasted my high school years.
I was too serious. I did not enjoy enough. I started early.
Maybe they are right. Maybe they are wrong.
Read my story, then tell me what you think.

It was October 2023 and I was in my first year of high school here in Japan.
I hated school and almost every subject, even the ones I used to like, probably because of the language barrier. It had only been a year since I moved.

I was scrolling social media like every other teenager, but something felt off.
My feed was full of people talking about how easy it was to get rich online. They made the whole thing sound simple enough that every minute I spent doing nothing felt like I was falling behind, even though I was only sixteen.

The first thing I tried was dropshipping.

I bought an online course, spent the rest of my savings on Shopify, a domain, and ads, and then spent months getting stuck and seeing no result. After four or five months, I cancelled everything and told myself maybe it was not for me.

The guy who sold me the course was probably still making money. I was left with the lesson.

Then came the summer of 2024 and I tried Amazon FBA.

I asked my dad for two thousand dollars so I could build a small brand. He agreed. I found a supplier on Alibaba, picked water bottles, and aimed for the US market.

The products reached Amazon warehouses. I was officially a brand owner at seventeen.

I waited for sales. I got one. Then nothing.

After months of staring at a blank dashboard, I paid Amazon to throw the inventory away so I could stop the extra costs. I still owe my dad for that mistake.

Then it was January 2025 and I decided to try content creation.

I made an account on X, thinking I would write about programming and AI. Later I found people talking about building products quickly and making money from them. That pulled me into the world of vibe coding.

I made a meme site called Snapmeme and posted about it. Every post got a handful of views. Nobody really cared, so eventually I gave up on that too.

I did not stop posting, though. Writing publicly still felt useful even when almost nobody saw it.

At some point I decided I should actually learn how to program.

I bought a long Python course and started it, but then I saw more people talking about money made from quick MVPs and abandoned the course before finishing. I went back to building without enough foundations.

I am still building. I still do not know whether this path is right or wrong. I still do not know whether it will work.

But I know this much now:

Just because someone on the internet got rich doing one thing does not mean you will fail at it.
And just because they succeeded does not mean you will too.

Figuring things out is a skill.

Most of the time, the reason I gave up was not that the idea was impossible. It was that I got stuck and did not know how to keep going.

Even with all the failed attempts, I gained something real. I learned how to use tools I would never have touched otherwise. Shopify, WordPress, GitHub, VS Code, Xcode, AI tools, design tools, and more all entered my life because I kept trying things.

So was it worth it?

I still do not have a clean answer.

But I do think those failures gave me curiosity, the courage to start, and a better understanding of how the internet sells dreams.

And maybe that is worth something.
